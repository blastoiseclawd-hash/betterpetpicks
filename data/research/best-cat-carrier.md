## Pattern Findings

### The Two Failure Modes
1. **Front-loading a scared cat.** Cats lock their legs against the front opening. An owner wrestling a panicking cat into a front-load carrier is the most common scene in a vet office parking lot. Top-loading carriers (Petmate Two-Door, Sherpa, Sleepypod) solve this — you lower the cat in from above, gravity helps.
2. **Soft-sided carrier used in a car crash.** Soft carriers are fine for airline travel and vet visits. They offer zero crash protection. For cross-country car travel or long drives on highways, a crash-tested carrier (Sleepypod Air) or a hard-sided kennel anchored with a seatbelt is the real answer.

### The Acclimation Shortcut Nobody Takes
The single biggest improvement you can make to vet-visit anxiety is leaving the carrier out at home year-round with the door open. Put a soft blanket inside. Add catnip or a favorite toy. The carrier stops being "the thing that means vet" and starts being furniture. After 2-4 weeks of voluntary use, most cats will enter on their own on vet-visit day.

Competitors describe this as optional. In owner feedback, it is the single intervention that changes everything about carrier use.

### Airline Requirements (The Actual Constraints)
Most airlines require:
- Under-seat dimensions around 18" L × 11" W × 11" H (varies slightly by airline and aircraft)
- Soft-sided or collapsible carriers generally preferred for in-cabin
- Weight of cat plus carrier typically under 20 lb
- Current health certificate from a vet within 10 days of travel

Sherpa's "Guaranteed on Board" program is not marketing — it actually reimburses the ticket if a gate agent rejects the carrier for being oversized. This is a real feature that matters for people who have been through a gate-check.

### Hard vs Soft vs Foldable
- **Hard-sided kennel** (Petmate): best for car rides, vet visits, cats that chew/claw through fabric. Can't be airplane carry-on.
- **Soft-sided** (Sherpa, Henkelion, Sleepypod): airline-friendly, lower profile, more comfortable for cat on lap. Less crash protection.
- **Foldable hard-sided** (SportPet): compromise — pops open when needed, folds flat for storage. Good for vet visits, not airline carry-on (dimensions too tall).

### Cat-Size-to-Carrier Mismatch
A cat needs to be able to **stand up, turn around, and lie down comfortably.** That is also the airline standard. Under-sizing a carrier is the most common fit mistake — people buy the smallest option to meet airline dimensions and the cat cannot turn around. For cats 12+ lb, the "medium" Sherpa or equivalent is usually undersized. Go up one size and check dimensions against the airline.

### The Claw-Grip Anchor Problem
Cats scared of carriers often grip the lip of the opening with their front claws while their back legs brace against the door frame. Pulling them makes the panic worse. The solution is to tip the carrier on end (front door facing up), let the cat slide down by gravity, and close the door. Top-loading carriers skip this whole scene.

### Crash Testing Matters for Long Drives
The Center for Pet Safety (CPS) independently crash-tests pet carriers. Very few pass. The Sleepypod Air and Sleepypod Mobile Pet Bed are the two that consistently do. For cats who ride in cars frequently or on long highway trips, this is a real safety feature, not a premium gimmick.

## Angles Competitors Miss

1. **Leave the carrier out year-round.** This is the single highest-value intervention, missing from most articles.
2. **Top-loading solves the leg-lock problem.** Skip the door-wrestling match entirely.
3. **"Airline approved" is not a standard.** Every airline's dimensions differ. Check the exact airline before buying.
4. **Crash testing exists.** Most carriers are not crash-tested. For frequent car travel, it's a real differentiator.
5. **Oversizing by one is usually right.** People buy too small to meet airline rules and the cat cannot turn around.
