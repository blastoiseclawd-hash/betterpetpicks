## Key Pattern Findings

### Two Definitions of "Indestructible"
The word conflates two different problems:
1. **Foam longevity** — the bed's foam does not flatten under a heavy dog over 1-3 years. Solved by high-density foam (40+ ILD) + tough cover.
2. **Chew resistance** — the bed survives actual destruction by teeth. Solved by elevated cot-style construction with no soft materials.

These are different products. Articles that blend them mislead the reader.

### Foam Density / ILD
"High-density memory foam" without a number is usually marketing. Real density specs are measured in pounds per cubic foot (PCF) or ILD:
- Quality orthopedic: 4+ PCF, 40-50 ILD
- Mid-grade: 2-3 PCF, 25-35 ILD (compresses within a year)
- Cheap: under 2 PCF, under 20 ILD (compresses in months)

If a listing doesn't publish density, assume it's mid-grade or cheap. Premium orthopedic brands publish the numbers because they compete on them.

### The Crate-vs-Bed Decision
For truly destructive dogs, the right answer is almost never "better bed." It's a crate during alone-time plus behavior work with a professional. A $275 bed destroyed in a week doesn't solve the underlying anxiety. Articles that sell "indestructible" as the solution to severe separation anxiety are solving the wrong problem.

### Replacement Cycle Math
A dog that destroys a $60 bed every 3 months = $240/year. A $275 bed that lasts 3 years = $92/year. But this math only works if the $275 bed actually lasts. For casual chewers the math works; for destructive chewers, no bed lasts.

### Review Variance Is Information
A 3.4-star bed with 4,000 reviews tells a real story. The one-stars cluster around specific failure modes (zipper seams, cover tearing after 3 weeks, foam compression). Don't dismiss low-star reviews — they describe the failure mode you might experience.
