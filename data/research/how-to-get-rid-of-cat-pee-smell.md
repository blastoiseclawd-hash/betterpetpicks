## The Science (Key Research)

### Cat Urine Composition
- Urea, urobilin/urobilinogin, uric acid, sodium, creatinine, pheromones, ~5 bacterial strains
- **Felinine**: sulfur-containing amino acid metabolite unique to cats — creates the distinctive cat urine odor
- Bacteria break down urea into ammonia (immediate smell)
- Further decomposition releases **thiols** (same compounds in skunk spray)
- **Uric acid**: not water-soluble, bonds tightly to surfaces, 6-year half-life
- Uric acid crystals reform when exposed to humidity — explains "phantom smell" returns

### Why Standard Cleaners Fail
- Vinegar, baking soda, hydrogen peroxide, chlorine → remove water-soluble components (urea, urobilin) only
- Cannot break down uric acid crystals
- After humidity exposure, crystals reform and smell returns
- Humans may not detect it, but cats can → re-marking cycle

### Why Enzymatic Cleaners Work
- Enzymes break uric acid into CO2 + ammonia → both evaporate as gases
- This is permanent removal, not masking
- Must air-dry naturally (heat interferes with enzyme action)
- Products: Nature's Miracle, Rocco & Roxie, Angry Orange, Anti-Icky Poo, Nok Out, Urine Off

## Surface-Specific Methods

### Carpet
1. Blot immediately (outside in, never push deeper)
2. Rinse with cold water, blot with wet/dry vacuum
3. Soak (not spray) with enzymatic cleaner — must penetrate padding
4. Wait 10-15 minutes, blot
5. Air dry completely
6. Repeat for old stains
- NEVER use steam cleaner (heat sets stain)
- Severe cases: replace carpet padding, possibly subfloor

### Hardwood Floors
- Wipe fresh urine immediately
- Apply enzymatic cleaner briefly (minimal soaking to prevent warping)
- Let dry completely between applications
- Sealed wood responds better than unsealed
- May require professional refinishing for deep stains

### Mattress
- Blot with towels
- Spray enzymatic cleaner generously
- Wait 15 minutes, blot
- Layer clean towels, replace daily until dry
- Multiple applications usually needed
- Install waterproof mattress protector afterward

### Upholstery/Furniture
- Remove and launder covers when possible
- For foam cushions: inject cleaner deep into foam (large-bore needle technique)
- Address wooden frame parts separately
- Waterproof covers post-cleaning

### Clothing
- Rinse in cool water (never hot)
- Wash with detergent + 1 cup baking soda OR 1/4 cup white vinegar
- If smell persists, add enzymatic cleaner and rewash
- Air-dry only (dryer heat sets odor)

### Concrete/Subfloor
- Most porous surface — urine soaks deep
- Multiple enzymatic cleaner applications
- May need concrete sealer after treatment
- Common issue under removed carpet

## Critical Mistakes

1. **Ammonia-based cleaners**: Cat urine contains ammonia → smells like a marking spot → triggers re-marking
2. **Steam cleaners**: Heat bonds proteins to surface, permanently sets stain
3. **Hot water**: Same problem as steam — sets the stain
4. **Pouring water on stain**: Spreads urine deeper and wider into padding/subfloor
5. **Not enough product**: Enzymatic cleaner must reach the same depth as the urine
6. **Not air-drying**: Enzymes need natural drying time to complete the chemical reaction
7. **Expired cleaner**: Enzymes degrade over time — check dates

## Finding Hidden Spots

- **Black light (UV light)**: Cat urine fluoresces under UV — scan walls, baseboards, furniture legs, carpet edges
- Best used in complete darkness for visibility
- Common hiding spots: behind furniture, under beds, along baseboards, laundry piles, shoes
- Mark spots with painter's tape before turning lights back on

## When to Replace vs. Clean

- **Clean**: Fresh stains, isolated incidents, hard/sealed surfaces
- **Replace**: Carpet padding (absorbs more than carpet), heavily soaked subfloor, old foam mattresses with repeated soaking
- **Professional help**: Multiple areas, recurring smell despite treatment, can't find the source

## Behavioral Context (Brief — Not YMYL)

Common reasons cats pee outside the box (reference only, not medical advice):
- Dirty litter box (most common)
- Wrong litter type or depth
- Box location issues (too exposed, too far)
- Medical issues (UTI, kidney disease) — "see your vet" language only
- Stress or territorial marking
- Not enough boxes (rule of thumb: one per cat + one)
