## Why this is `expert-consensus`, not `hands-on` or `spec-based`

The page is a category-education guide. It does NOT rank or recommend specific products as buyer-best picks; it explains what makes a litter genuinely low-dust, names representative examples by material category, and routes readers to /best-cat-litter for purchase decisions. The substantive claims are drawn from:

- 60+ owner reports across Reddit, enthusiast forums, and retailer reviews (counted across the 20 candidate products in the underlying wide-net pool)
- 5 expert review publications (Cats.com, Tuft & Paw, Kinship, Catster, Chewy editorial)
- Manufacturer dust claims (treated skeptically — see "the 99% dust-free credibility problem" section)

`pageLevelClaimLabel: expert-consensus` is correct because the work is synthesis of multiple expert publications + cross-publication owner-report consensus, not desk-conducted hands-on testing and not pure manufacturer-spec orientation. No "we tested in our home" language appears on the page. No product is given a hands-on label.

## Cross-publication divergence

- **Cats.com vs Reddit on Okocat.** Cats.com names "ökocat Natural Wood Cat Litter" as its #1 overall dust-free pick. Reddit complaints cluster around Okocat *Featherweight* (a different formula in the same product line). The variant naming creates buyer confusion. Page-implication: name the variant explicitly when referencing Okocat (the natural wood / original line, not the featherweight); call out the variant trap as buyer-protection information.

- **Manufacturer "99% dust-free" claims vs owner reports.** Nearly every brand claims 99% or 99.9% dust-free. Owner reports across Reddit show this claim is frequently disputed for clay litters: "Every search brings up a trillion products saying dust free... half of the people say no dust and the other half say it's the dustiest worst litter ever" (recurring r/CatAdvice complaint). Page-implication: do not repeat manufacturer dust-free claims uncritically; lead the page with the credibility problem so readers calibrate expectations before reading the rest.

- **Vet-tech caution on organic-base litters for asthmatic cats.** Most "dust-free" advocacy pushes readers toward tofu, grass, paper, or pine. The well-upvoted Reddit comment from a vet tech captured above warns that for a *diagnosed* asthmatic cat, switching to organic-base litters can worsen symptoms because of plant-protein reactions, and a "good clay" may actually be safer pending allergy testing. Page-implication: surface this caveat prominently. Do not let the page's general "go natural" tendency override individualized vet guidance.

- **PrettyLitter dust degradation over time.** Multiple Reddit threads report PrettyLitter starts low-dust but degrades into a fine dust as crystals break down across the monthly cycle: "Pretty Litter was fine at first but we felt it started to get dusty after a while" — contradicts PrettyLitter's marketing. Page-implication: name this honestly; not all "low-dust" stays low-dust over a 30-day cycle.

## Failure-modes / "when not to buy" notes for the explainer

These don't render as a per-product table (no products are ranked), but inform the page's decision-orientation section:

- **Cat rejection of pellets.** Pine, paper, tofu, and grass pellets are categorically lower-dust than clay, but cats accustomed to clay frequently reject pellet textures on first introduction. Reddit consensus: "tried pine pellets but she protested those" is a recurring outcome. Page should set expectations: a 1-2 week transition with mixed bedding is normal; sudden rejection is the most common reason owners give up on pellet litters.

- **Tracking trade-off.** Most genuinely-low-dust litters (Pidan, SmartCat Grass, Sustainably Yours) track more than fine clay. The trade is "less airborne dust, more litter on the floor." For dust-sensitive readers (asthma, allergies, Sphynx cats), the trade is usually worth it; for a reader whose primary complaint is "litter on my floor," the answer is different (route to /low-tracking-cat-litter).

- **Cost barrier.** Dust-free standouts are 3-6x the cost-per-pound of standard clay (Pidan ~$4.50/lb, Tuft & Paw ~$3.13/lb vs Dr. Elsey's at $0.52/lb, per the prior research artifact's price-per-pound calculations). Multi-cat households scale this cost badly. Page should not pretend dust-free is free.

- **Tofu litter ingestion warning.** A recurring Reddit anecdote: cats sometimes try to eat tofu litter. Most cats don't, but some do, and the food-grade ingredient profile makes the appeal real. For households with eating-disorder or pica-prone cats, tofu is the wrong answer.

- **Pine allergy edge case.** Some asthmatic cats react to pine litter specifically. Vet-flagged caveat — not common, but documented enough in Reddit threads to warrant a sentence.
